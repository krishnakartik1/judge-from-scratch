## Findings

**Training worked, but DPO's value is uneven.** In-dist κ improved
0.481 → 0.647 → 0.682 across baseline → SFT → DPO. SFT is the bigger
jump (+0.166); DPO adds a modest +0.035. DPO's clearest wins are on
tie cases (−0.056 → 0.359 — SFT actually goes worse-than-chance on
ties; DPO recovers) and subtle-bias discrimination (0.743 → 0.890).
Position-bias halved (21% → 8–9% in-dist) and self-consistency rose
from 74% → 83% — both clean training wins. SFT lands at the
realistic 0.68 in-dist target from primer Appendix C; DPO matches
it. The stretch target (0.76) is not reached.

**DPO regresses on OOD generalization.** SFT generalizes to held-out
religion (κ=0.695) better than DPO (κ=0.643). This is the opposite
of the primer's prediction. The likely explanation: synthesized hard
negatives encoded patterns specific to the 10 in-dist bias categories,
and DPO learned to discriminate those patterns rather than
bias-in-general. This is a genuinely interesting finding, not a
failure — it tells you that DPO preference data needs OOD diversity,
not just in-dist difficulty. The same pattern shows up on
position-bias-OOD (SFT 11.7% vs DPO 16.7%): DPO is more confident
in-dist and that confidence doesn't transfer.

**Tracked-vs-alternate is at the task's structural floor.** κ stays
at 0.12–0.20 across all three models on the hardest bucket — capped
by 220 training pairs at BBQ's structural ceiling (decision in the
"Open threads" section: this is supply-bound, not a heuristic
problem). The synth negatives weren't designed to target this bucket
specifically. Honest acknowledgment: this slice needs either more
data (a different question structure) or a different training
approach to improve meaningfully. DPO actually regresses here
(0.197 → 0.119), confirming the synth-negatives-overfit story above.

**The SFT-only checkpoint may be better for OOD use cases.** DPO
wins in-dist (0.682 vs 0.647) and ties (0.359 vs −0.056). SFT wins
OOD (0.695 vs 0.643) and tracked-vs-alternate (0.197 vs 0.119).
Users evaluating bias categories outside the BBQ training set should
consider the SFT-only checkpoint. The published model card surfaces
this as a recommendation, not a hedge — both checkpoints will be
released, and the SFT artifact's card will explicitly position it
as "use this if your bias categories are out-of-distribution
relative to BBQ's training set."

**Truncation bias in eval is real and measurable.** The first
run-all at `max_tokens=384` (sized for the baseline) inflated
SFT/DPO κ by 0.06–0.18 across buckets by systematically dropping
the harder cases — pairs where the trained model wrote longer
reasoning are the same pairs where it was struggling most.
DPO-OOD κ shifted from 0.820 (preview, 9.9% truncation) to 0.643
(headline, 0.3% truncation): −0.177 from a methodology fix alone.
This is the load-bearing methodological lesson of Stage 8 — token
budget for eval must accommodate the trained model's output
distribution, not just the baseline's, and truncation that
correlates with case difficulty is a silent selection bias on the
metric you actually care about. The preview file is retained at
`eval/results/stage8_final_20260508T115420.md` as a reference for
how the bias presents.
